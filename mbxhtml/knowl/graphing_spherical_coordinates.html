EXTRA 19 Recall that \(\phi\) (“phi”) is the angle down from the \(z\) axis, \(\theta\) (“theta”) is the angle counterclockwise from the \(x\)-axis in the \(xy\)-plane, and \(\rho\) (“rho”) is the distance from the origin. Review <<Unresolved xref, reference "derive_spherical_coordinates"; check spelling or use "provisional" attribute>> if you need a refresher.\larsonfive{ EXTRA 20 See Larson 11.7:89–94, 111–114.}Consider the spherical coordinates transformation as defined in \stewarts{ \begin{equation*} \vec T(\rho,\phi,\theta)=(\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta,\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta,\rho\cos\phi), \end{equation*}

\bmw{ \begin{equation*} \vec T(\rho,\phi,\theta)=(\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta,\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta,\rho\cos\phi), \end{equation*}

\larsonfive{ \begin{equation*} \vec T(\rho,\theta,\phi)=(\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta,\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta,\rho\cos\phi), \end{equation*} which could also be written as \begin{align*} x\amp =\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta\\ y\amp =\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta\\ z\amp =\rho\cos\phi \end{align*}

Graphing this transformation requires \(3+3=6\) dimensions. In this problem we'll construct parts of this graph by graphing various surfaces. We did something similar for the polar coordinate transformation in problem 6.1.6.
