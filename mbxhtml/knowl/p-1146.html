EXTRA 9 Watch a YouTube Video. Suppose a curve \(\vec r(t)\) has the frame \(\vec T(t)\text{,}\), \(\vec N(t)\text{,}\), and \(\vec B(t)\text{.}\). Prove that \(\dfrac{d\vec B}{ds}\) is either parallel to \(\vec N\text{,}\), or points opposite \(\vec N\text{.}\). Here are some steps.

  • Why is \(\dfrac{d\vec B}{ds}\) orthogonal to \(\vec B\text{?}\) Hint

    How long is \(\vec B\text{?}\) See Theorem 9.4.1.

  • We know \(\vec B=\vec T\times \vec N\text{.}\) Compute the derivative of both sides using the product rule and explain why \(\frac{d\vec T}{ds}\times \vec N\) cancels out. Then explain why \(\dfrac{d\vec B}{ds}\) is orthogonal to \(\vec T\text{.}\)

  • If \(\dfrac{d\vec B}{ds}\) is orthogonal to both \(\vec B\) and \(\vec T\) why must it be either parallel or anti-parallel to \(\vec N\text{?}\)
