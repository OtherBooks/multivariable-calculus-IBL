Consider again \(f(x,y)=9-x^2-y^2\) and \(\vec r(t)=(2\cos t, 3\sin t)\text{.}\)

  1. Compute both \(Df(x,y)\) and \(D\vec r(t)\) as matrices. One should have two columns. The other should have one column (but two rows).

We can write the temperature at any time \(t\) symbolically as \(f(r(t))\text{.}\) First semester calculus suggests the derivative should be the product \((f(\vec r(t))) ' = f'(\vec r(t))\vec r'(t)\text{.}\)

  1. Write this using \(D\) notation instead of prime notation.

  2. Compute the matrix product \(DfD\vec r\text{,}\) and then substitute \(x=2\cos t\) and \(y=3\sin t\text{.}\)

  3. What is the change in temperature with respect to time at \(t=\pi/4\text{?}\) Is it positive or negative? Compare with the previous exercise.
