Section10.2Area and the Line-Integral

  • motive, and practice performing line-integrals

In first semester calculus, we learned that the area under a function \(f(x)\) above the \(x\)-axis is given by \(A = \int_a^b f(x) dx\text{.}\) The quantity \(dA= f(x) dx\) represents a small bit of area whose length is \(dx\) and whose height is \(f(x)\text{.}\) To get the total area, we just added up the little bits of area, which is why \begin{equation*} A=\int dA = \int_a^b f(x) dx. \end{equation*}

Definition10.2.2Line Integral

EXTRA 5 The line integral is also called the path integral, contour integral, or curve integral. Let \(f\) be a function and let \(C\) be a piecewise smooth curve whose parametrization is \(\vec r(t)\) for \(t\in[a,b]\text{.}\) We'll require that the composition \(f(\vec r(t))\) be continuous for all \(t\in [a,b]\text{.}\) Then we define the line integral of \(f\) over \(C\) to be the integral \begin{equation*} \int_C f ds = \int_a^b f(\vec r(t))\frac{ds}{dt}dt = \int_a^b f(\vec r(t))\left|\frac{d\vec r}{dt}\right|dt. \end{equation*}

Notice that this definition suggests the following four steps. These four steps are the key to computing any line integral. EXTRA 6 When we ask you to set up a line integral, it means that you should do steps 1–3, so that you get an integral with a single variable and with bounds that you could plug into a computer or complete by hand.

  1. Start by getting a parametrization \(\vec r(t)\) for \(a\leq t\leq b\) of the curve \(C\text{.}\)

  2. Find the speed by computing the velocity \(\dfrac{d\vec r}{dt}\) and then the speed \(\left|\dfrac{d\vec r}{dt}\right|\text{.}\)

  3. Multiply \(f\) by the speed, and replace each \(x\text{,}\) \(y\text{,}\) and/or \(z\) with what it equals in terms of \(t\text{.}\)

  4. EXTRA 7 You should use the Sage line integral calculator to check all your answers.Integrate the product from the previous step. Practice doing this by hand on every exercise, unless it specifically says to use technology. Some of the integrals are impossible to do by hand.

Work, flow, circulation, and flux are all examples of line integrals. Remember that work, flow, and circulation are \begin{equation*} W=\int_C (\vec F\cdot \vec T)ds =\int_C (M,N)\cdot(dx,dy) = \int_C Mdx+Ndy, \end{equation*} while the formula for flux is \begin{equation*} \Phi=\int_C (\vec F\cdot \vec n)ds =\int_C (M,N)\cdot(dy,-dx) = \int_C Mdy+Ndx. \end{equation*}

Do you see how these are both the line integral of a function \(f = \vec F\cdot \vec T\) or \(f=\vec F\cdot \vec n\) along a curve \(C\text{.}\) The function \(f\) inside the integrand does not have to represent the height of a sheet. We'll use it to represent lots of things. Let's practice two more work/flux exercises, to sharpen our skills with these concepts.
